Ratio Simplification Rules
Unit digit of a power depends only on the unit digit of the base and cycles in a pattern of 4. The GRE asks this frequently: "What is the units digit of 7⁴³?"
Method: Divide exponent by 4. Use remainder to find position in cycle. Remainder 0 → use cycle position 4.
Even/Odd and Consecutive Integer rules — tested constantly on the GRE in both QC and problem-solving questions.
Terminating vs Non-Terminating Decimals — a fraction terminates if and only if its denominator (in lowest terms) has no prime factors other than 2 and 5.

Trailing zeroes come from factors of 10 = 2×5. Count pairs of (2,5) in the prime factorisation. The 5s are almost always limiting.

Leading zeroes in a decimal like 0.00347 — the number of leading zeroes after the decimal point equals the negative power of 10.
Legendre's Formula: The number of times prime p divides n! is:
⌊n/p⌋ + ⌊n/p²⌋ + ⌊n/p³⌋ + … (stop when p^k > n)
Numbers between 0 and 1 behave counter-intuitively — the GRE exploits this constantly in quantitative comparison questions.
Pythagorean triplets: Integer right-triangle side combinations. Memorise the first 4 unique sets and their multiples — they appear in almost every GRE geometry question.
Key multiples to know
Similar triangles have equal corresponding angles and proportional sides. Three similarity conditions: AA (two equal angles), SAS (two sides proportional + included angle equal), SSS (all three sides proportional).
